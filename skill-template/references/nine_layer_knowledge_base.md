# FinGraph Nine-Layer Knowledge Base

## Purpose

This reference explains the FinGraph macro-financial framework for readers and models with little financial background. It turns scattered news and data into a structured view of how the global financial system affects U.S. risk assets, especially Nasdaq, QQQ, and technology equities.

FinGraph uses nine layers. The first eight layers explain where macro-financial forces originate and how they propagate. The ninth layer turns that evidence into an asset-decision view:

1. Currency layer
2. Central bank layer
3. Fiscal layer
4. Industry layer
5. Corporate layer
6. Geopolitical layer
7. Social layer
8. Market layer
9. Asset decision layer

The key idea is simple: asset prices are not moved by news headlines alone. They are moved by how events change expected cash flows, discount rates, liquidity, risk appetite, policy reaction functions, and long-term confidence.

## Core Concepts For Beginners

### Cash flow

An asset is valuable because it may produce future cash flows. For a company, this means revenue, profit, and free cash flow. For a bond, this means interest and principal payments. For a commodity, this means supply-demand scarcity and storage value.

### Discount rate

Money in the future is worth less than money today. Investors discount future cash flows back to the present. When interest rates rise, the discount rate rises, and long-duration assets such as technology stocks often become less valuable.

### Liquidity

Liquidity means how much money and balance-sheet capacity is available to buy assets. When liquidity is abundant, investors can take more risk. When liquidity tightens, even good assets can fall because buyers become scarce.

### Risk appetite

Risk appetite describes how willing investors are to own risky assets. It rises when growth is strong, inflation is controlled, policy is supportive, and markets trust the future. It falls during wars, banking stress, inflation shocks, or recession fears.

### Policy reaction function

A policy reaction function is how a central bank or government is likely to respond to changing conditions. For example, if inflation rises, the Federal Reserve may keep rates high. If unemployment rises sharply, it may become more willing to cut rates.

### Time horizon

Events have different horizons:

- Short term: days to weeks. Usually market sentiment, positioning, and headlines.
- Medium term: months. Usually policy expectations, earnings revisions, and macro data trends.
- Long term: years. Usually fiscal sustainability, productivity, demographics, industrial capacity, and geopolitical alignment.
- Structural: decade-scale changes to the system.

## Layer 1: Currency Layer

### Plain-language definition

The currency layer studies the role of the U.S. dollar and global money flows. The U.S. dollar is not just the currency of the United States. It is also the main currency for global trade, debt, reserves, commodities, and financial settlement.

### Why it matters

If the dollar is strong, global financial conditions often tighten. Countries and companies that borrow in dollars face higher repayment burdens. Commodities priced in dollars may become more expensive for non-U.S. buyers. A strong dollar can also reduce the overseas earnings of U.S. multinational companies when translated back into dollars.

If the dollar weakens in an orderly way, global liquidity may improve and risk assets can benefit. If the dollar weakens because confidence in U.S. institutions collapses, that is a very different and more dangerous signal.

### Key concepts

- Dollar index: a measure of the dollar against major currencies.
- Reserve currency: a currency central banks hold as reserves.
- Trade invoicing: the currency used to price international trade.
- Dollar funding: the ability of banks and firms outside the U.S. to borrow dollars.
- Safe haven demand: demand for dollars during crises.
- De-dollarization: gradual reduction in reliance on the dollar.
- Foreign exchange reserves: assets held by central banks, often in dollars and U.S. Treasuries.

### Important indicators

- DXY or broad dollar index
- U.S. real yields
- Fed policy expectations
- Global foreign exchange reserves
- Treasury holdings by foreign official institutions
- Cross-currency basis swaps
- Gold reserves
- SWIFT payment currency shares
- Commodity prices in dollars

### How to interpret signals

Dollar up because U.S. growth is stronger than the rest of the world:
Often mixed. It can reflect U.S. resilience, but it can pressure emerging markets and multinational earnings.

Dollar up because markets fear a crisis:
Usually risk-off. Investors may sell risky assets and buy dollars or Treasuries.

Dollar down because the Fed is expected to cut rates:
Often positive for risk assets if recession risk is low.

Dollar down because trust in U.S. debt or institutions is falling:
Potentially dangerous. Gold, inflation hedges, and non-dollar assets may gain relative importance.

### Main transmission channels

Currency layer affects:

- Central bank layer through imported inflation and financial conditions.
- Fiscal layer through foreign demand for Treasuries.
- Corporate layer through overseas revenue translation and funding costs.
- Market layer through global liquidity and risk appetite.
- Geopolitical layer through sanctions, reserves, and settlement systems.

### Common mistakes

- Treating every dollar decline as bullish.
- Ignoring why the dollar is moving.
- Looking only at DXY and ignoring global dollar funding stress.
- Assuming de-dollarization is immediate rather than slow and path-dependent.

## Layer 2: Central Bank Layer

### Plain-language definition

The central bank layer studies monetary policy. In the U.S., the Federal Reserve influences short-term interest rates, liquidity, inflation expectations, credit conditions, and risk appetite.

### Why it matters

The Fed does not directly set stock prices, but it heavily influences the discount rate used to value stocks and bonds. Technology stocks often depend on future earnings, so their valuations are especially sensitive to real interest rates.

### Key concepts

- Policy rate: the short-term interest rate target.
- Inflation: the rate at which prices rise.
- Core inflation: inflation excluding volatile food and energy.
- PCE: the Fed's preferred inflation measure.
- Employment mandate: the Fed also cares about jobs.
- Real rate: nominal interest rate minus inflation expectations.
- Yield curve: interest rates across different maturities.
- QE: central bank asset purchases that add liquidity.
- QT: central bank balance-sheet runoff that removes liquidity.
- Forward guidance: communication about future policy.
- Hawkish: more focused on inflation, tighter policy.
- Dovish: more focused on growth/employment, easier policy.

### Important indicators

- Federal funds target range
- Fed funds futures
- CPI, core CPI
- PCE, core PCE
- Nonfarm payrolls
- Unemployment rate
- Wage growth
- Job openings
- 2-year Treasury yield
- 10-year Treasury yield
- Real yields
- Fed balance sheet
- FOMC statements, minutes, dot plot, speeches

### How to interpret signals

Inflation high and labor market strong:
The Fed may stay restrictive. This can pressure long-duration equities.

Inflation falling and labor market stable:
Goldilocks conditions. This can support risk assets.

Inflation falling because demand is collapsing:
Not automatically bullish. Rate cuts may come with recession risk.

Long-term yields rising while the Fed is near pause:
May indicate fiscal concerns, term premium, or stronger growth.

### Main transmission channels

Central bank layer affects:

- Currency layer through interest-rate differentials.
- Fiscal layer through government interest cost.
- Corporate layer through borrowing costs and valuation multiples.
- Market layer through liquidity and discount rates.
- Social layer through employment, housing affordability, and credit costs.

### Common mistakes

- Assuming rate cuts are always bullish.
- Ignoring the reason for rate cuts.
- Watching only the policy rate and ignoring real rates.
- Confusing lower inflation with strong growth.

## Layer 3: Fiscal Layer

### Plain-language definition

The fiscal layer studies government spending, taxation, deficits, debt, and Treasury issuance. Monetary policy is about the central bank. Fiscal policy is about the government budget.

### Why it matters

The U.S. government funds deficits by issuing debt. If debt grows faster than the economy, investors may demand higher yields. Higher yields can pressure equity valuations and raise interest costs for the government, companies, and households.

### Key concepts

- Deficit: government spending minus revenue in a year.
- Debt: accumulated past deficits.
- Debt-to-GDP: debt compared with economic size.
- Interest expense: cost of servicing government debt.
- Treasury issuance: new government bonds sold to investors.
- Auction demand: how strongly investors buy new Treasuries.
- Term premium: extra yield investors demand for holding long-term bonds.
- Fiscal dominance: central bank policy becomes constrained by government debt.
- Debt ceiling: legal cap on U.S. federal borrowing.

### Important indicators

- Federal deficit
- Debt-to-GDP
- Net interest expense
- Treasury auction bid-to-cover ratios
- Foreign Treasury holdings
- 10-year and 30-year Treasury yields
- Term premium estimates
- Tax receipts
- Government outlays
- CBO projections
- Treasury refunding announcements

### How to interpret signals

Deficit rises during recession:
Can be stabilizing, but increases debt.

Deficit rises during strong growth:
Can be inflationary and may pressure bond yields.

Treasury supply rises while demand is weak:
May push long yields higher.

Interest expense grows rapidly:
Can reduce fiscal flexibility and increase market concern.

### Main transmission channels

Fiscal layer affects:

- Central bank layer through inflation and rate pressure.
- Currency layer through confidence in U.S. debt.
- Market layer through bond yields and risk premiums.
- Social layer through taxes, transfers, and public services.
- Industry layer through subsidies, infrastructure, defense, and industrial policy.

### Common mistakes

- Treating debt level alone as a timing signal.
- Ignoring whether debt funds consumption or productive investment.
- Ignoring maturity structure and interest expense.
- Assuming the U.S. has no constraint because it issues the reserve currency.

## Layer 4: Industry Layer

### Plain-language definition

The industry layer studies the productive structure of the economy: manufacturing, energy, technology infrastructure, supply chains, labor skills, and productivity.

### Why it matters

Stock prices ultimately need real economic capacity behind them. If industry productivity rises, companies can grow profits without creating inflation. If supply chains are fragile or manufacturing capacity is weak, shocks can become inflationary and politically destabilizing.

### Key concepts

- Productivity: output per unit of input.
- Reindustrialization: rebuilding domestic manufacturing capacity.
- Supply chain resilience: ability to withstand disruptions.
- Capex: investment in equipment, factories, data centers, or infrastructure.
- PMI: survey of manufacturing or service activity.
- Semiconductor supply chain: chips, equipment, fabs, packaging, and materials.
- AI infrastructure: data centers, GPUs, power, cooling, networking.
- Energy intensity: how much energy production requires.

### Important indicators

- Manufacturing PMI
- Industrial production
- Capacity utilization
- Durable goods orders
- Construction spending
- Semiconductor sales
- AI data center capex
- Electricity demand
- Energy prices
- Manufacturing employment
- Trade balance
- Supply chain stress indexes

### How to interpret signals

Strong productivity growth:
Usually positive. It can support earnings and reduce inflation pressure.

AI capex boom with real revenue growth:
Positive for corporate and industry layers.

AI capex boom without monetization:
May create overinvestment risk.

Energy bottlenecks:
Can limit industrial expansion and raise costs.

### Main transmission channels

Industry layer affects:

- Corporate layer through margins, revenue growth, and capex.
- Central bank layer through supply-side inflation or disinflation.
- Fiscal layer through subsidies and industrial policy.
- Geopolitical layer through supply-chain dependence.
- Social layer through jobs, wages, and regional inequality.

### Common mistakes

- Treating every industrial policy headline as immediately bullish.
- Ignoring energy and labor constraints.
- Ignoring the difference between revenue growth and speculative capex.
- Assuming reshoring is costless.

## Layer 5: Corporate Layer

### Plain-language definition

The corporate layer studies company earnings, margins, balance sheets, valuations, and the ability of firms to convert economic growth into shareholder value.

### Why it matters

Equity prices depend on two large forces: expected future profits and valuation multiples. A great company can still be a poor investment if expectations are too high. A weak macro environment can be offset if earnings growth is strong enough.

### Key concepts

- Revenue: sales.
- Gross margin: revenue minus cost of goods sold.
- Operating margin: profit from core business before interest and taxes.
- Net margin: final profit after all costs.
- EPS: earnings per share.
- Free cash flow: cash left after capital expenditures.
- ROIC: return on invested capital.
- Multiple: price investors pay for a unit of earnings or sales.
- Buybacks: companies repurchasing shares.
- Balance sheet: assets, liabilities, and equity.
- Magnificent 7: large U.S. technology leaders that heavily influence indexes.

### Important indicators

- Revenue growth
- EPS growth
- Free cash flow
- Gross and operating margins
- Forward P/E
- Earnings revisions
- Credit ratings
- Debt maturity schedule
- AI capex and AI revenue
- Cloud growth
- Semiconductor demand
- Buyback authorizations
- Index concentration

### How to interpret signals

Earnings growth broadens beyond mega-cap tech:
Positive market-health signal.

Index rises only because a few stocks rise:
Can indicate concentration risk.

Margins expand because of productivity:
Positive.

Margins expand because of temporary cost cuts:
May not be sustainable.

Capex rises faster than revenue:
Could be future growth investment or overbuild risk.

### Main transmission channels

Corporate layer affects:

- Market layer through earnings and valuations.
- Industry layer through investment and innovation.
- Fiscal layer through tax receipts.
- Social layer through employment and wages.
- Geopolitical layer through supply-chain exposure and export controls.

### Common mistakes

- Confusing great businesses with attractive valuations.
- Ignoring index concentration.
- Ignoring balance-sheet risk when rates are high.
- Treating AI narratives as equal to realized cash flow.

## Layer 6: Geopolitical Layer

### Plain-language definition

The geopolitical layer studies how conflict, sanctions, trade restrictions, alliances, wars, and strategic competition affect markets and economic systems.

### Why it matters

Geopolitical events can change energy prices, supply chains, defense spending, currency reserves, shipping routes, and investor risk premiums. Some shocks are temporary; others permanently change the structure of globalization.

### Key concepts

- Sanctions: restrictions on trade, finance, or technology.
- Export controls: limits on selling sensitive goods or technology.
- Tariffs: taxes on imports.
- Supply-chain fragmentation: movement from global efficiency to regional security.
- Risk premium: extra return demanded for uncertainty.
- Strategic chokepoint: a location critical to trade or energy shipping.
- Alliance structure: geopolitical blocs that influence trade and technology flows.

### Important indicators

- Sanctions announcements
- Tariff changes
- Export control lists
- Shipping rates
- Oil and gas prices
- Defense spending
- Country risk spreads
- Taiwan Strait, South China Sea, Middle East, Red Sea, Russia-Ukraine indicators
- Semiconductor restrictions
- GDELT event intensity and tone

### How to interpret signals

Energy conflict escalates:
Can raise oil prices, inflation expectations, and Fed pressure.

Chip export controls tighten:
Mixed. May hurt some revenue but support domestic strategic investment.

Trade war escalates:
Can raise inflation and lower margins.

De-escalation:
Can reduce risk premium and support risk assets.

### Main transmission channels

Geopolitical layer affects:

- Currency layer through reserve behavior and sanctions.
- Industry layer through supply chains.
- Corporate layer through sales, margins, and regulatory risk.
- Central bank layer through inflation shocks.
- Market layer through volatility and risk premium.

### Common mistakes

- Treating every geopolitical headline as market-moving.
- Ignoring whether the event affects energy, chips, trade, or capital flows.
- Ignoring second-order effects on inflation and policy.
- Assuming markets price slow structural fragmentation immediately.

## Layer 7: Social Layer

### Plain-language definition

The social layer studies households, labor, inequality, political polarization, housing affordability, consumer balance sheets, and social stability.

### Why it matters

Markets often focus on companies and rates, but societies determine political constraints. If inequality, housing costs, or job insecurity rise too much, governments may respond with taxes, subsidies, tariffs, regulation, or deficit spending.

### Key concepts

- Real wage: wage adjusted for inflation.
- Consumer confidence: household optimism.
- Household debt service: share of income used for debt payments.
- Wealth effect: people spend more when asset prices rise.
- Inequality: uneven distribution of income or wealth.
- Political polarization: difficulty forming stable policy consensus.
- Housing affordability: ability of households to buy or rent homes.
- Labor participation: share of people working or looking for work.

### Important indicators

- Real wages
- Unemployment rate
- Labor force participation
- Consumer confidence
- Retail sales
- Credit card delinquencies
- Mortgage rates
- Rent and home prices
- Household debt service ratio
- Gini coefficient
- Election and policy risk indicators

### How to interpret signals

Strong wages with falling inflation:
Positive for consumption and social stability.

Strong wages with sticky inflation:
Can pressure the Fed to stay tight.

Rising delinquencies:
May indicate consumer stress.

Housing affordability crisis:
Can become political and fiscal pressure.

### Main transmission channels

Social layer affects:

- Fiscal layer through redistribution, subsidies, taxes, and spending.
- Central bank layer through employment and wage inflation.
- Corporate layer through consumption and labor costs.
- Market layer through political risk and sentiment.
- Geopolitical layer through domestic pressure for protectionism.

### Common mistakes

- Ignoring households because the stock market is company-focused.
- Treating low unemployment as always bullish.
- Ignoring wage-inflation tradeoffs.
- Ignoring political feedback from economic stress.

## Layer 8: Market Layer

### Plain-language definition

The market layer studies asset prices, valuations, volatility, sentiment, liquidity, positioning, breadth, credit spreads, and investor behavior.

### Why it matters

Markets are the final pricing layer. They translate all other layers into prices. Sometimes prices move before fundamentals because investors anticipate changes. Sometimes prices detach from fundamentals because liquidity and sentiment dominate.

### Key concepts

- Valuation: how expensive an asset is relative to earnings, cash flow, or book value.
- Volatility: size of price movements.
- VIX: market-implied equity volatility.
- Credit spread: extra yield on risky debt over Treasuries.
- Market breadth: how many stocks participate in a move.
- Positioning: how investors are currently allocated.
- Momentum: tendency for trends to continue for a time.
- Drawdown: decline from a peak.
- Equity risk premium: expected stock return over risk-free bonds.

### Important indicators

- Nasdaq, QQQ, SPY
- S&P 500 forward P/E
- Nasdaq valuation
- VIX
- Credit spreads
- High-yield spreads
- Market breadth
- Put/call ratios
- Fund flows
- Margin debt
- Treasury yields
- Gold, oil, bitcoin
- CFTC positioning

### How to interpret signals

Stocks up, breadth strong, credit spreads tight:
Generally healthy risk-on signal.

Stocks up, breadth narrow, valuations high:
Can indicate fragility.

VIX low with high geopolitical risk:
May indicate complacency.

Credit spreads widening while equities rise:
Potential warning signal.

### Main transmission channels

Market layer affects:

- Corporate layer through cost of capital and wealth effects.
- Social layer through household wealth and retirement accounts.
- Fiscal layer through tax receipts and crisis response.
- Central bank layer through financial conditions.
- Currency layer through capital flows.

### Common mistakes

- Treating price action as proof of fundamentals.
- Ignoring valuation because momentum is strong.
- Ignoring credit markets.
- Assuming low volatility means low risk.

## Layer 9: Asset Decision Layer

### Plain-language definition

The asset decision layer is the translation layer. It does not create new facts. It converts the evidence from the first eight layers into practical asset questions: which assets benefit, which assets are vulnerable, what time horizon matters, how strong is the evidence, and what would invalidate the view.

This layer is especially important for users who are not finance professionals. A macro event is not useful by itself until it is connected to assets such as QQQ, Nasdaq, SPY, TLT, DXY, gold, oil, credit, cash, or sector ETFs.

### Why it matters

Different assets respond to different mechanisms:

- QQQ and Nasdaq are sensitive to discount rates, AI earnings, margins, liquidity, and risk appetite.
- SPY is broader and reflects the overall U.S. earnings cycle, rates, credit, and policy confidence.
- TLT is sensitive to long-term Treasury yields, inflation expectations, fiscal supply, and recession risk.
- DXY reflects relative growth, relative rates, global dollar demand, and confidence in the dollar system.
- Gold responds to real yields, currency confidence, geopolitical risk, and central-bank reserve behavior.
- Oil responds to demand, supply, inventories, OPEC policy, war risk, and dollar strength.
- Credit spreads reflect default risk, liquidity, financial conditions, and recession probability.
- Cash becomes more attractive when yields are high, uncertainty is high, or risk assets have weak compensation.

### Key concepts

- Asset sensitivity: which macro variable an asset reacts to most.
- Duration: how far into the future an asset's cash flows or valuation assumptions extend.
- Convexity: whether an asset reacts more strongly as conditions become extreme.
- Risk premium: compensation investors demand for bearing uncertainty.
- Cross-asset confirmation: whether equities, bonds, FX, commodities, and credit tell the same story.
- Invalidation: evidence that would prove the current interpretation wrong.
- Positioning risk: whether too many investors already hold the same view.

### Important indicators

- QQQ, Nasdaq 100, SPY, equal-weight S&P 500
- 2-year, 10-year, and 30-year Treasury yields
- Real yields and breakeven inflation
- DXY and major FX pairs
- Gold, oil, copper, natural gas
- VIX and credit spreads
- Equity market breadth and sector leadership
- Earnings revisions and forward margins
- ETF flows, options positioning, and CFTC positioning

### How to interpret signals

Falling inflation with stable employment:
Often supportive for QQQ/SPY and TLT because discount-rate pressure falls without a recession shock.

Falling inflation because demand is collapsing:
Potentially negative for equities and positive for duration bonds, depending on credit stress and earnings risk.

Rising yields from stronger growth:
Mixed. Equities may tolerate it if earnings rise faster than discount rates.

Rising yields from fiscal stress or inflation fear:
Usually negative for long-duration equities and long bonds.

Oil spike from supply shock:
Can pressure equities through inflation expectations and margins, while supporting energy assets.

Dollar spike during global stress:
Often risk-off for global equities and commodities, even if it reflects U.S. relative strength.

### Main transmission channels

Asset decision layer receives from all eight analytical layers:

- Currency layer: dollar liquidity, FX translation, global funding.
- Central bank layer: policy rates, real yields, liquidity.
- Fiscal layer: Treasury supply, term premium, deficit credibility.
- Industry layer: productivity, energy, supply chains.
- Corporate layer: earnings, margins, capex, cash flow.
- Geopolitical layer: sanctions, war risk, reserve behavior, supply disruption.
- Social layer: consumption, labor, politics, regulation.
- Market layer: valuation, volatility, breadth, positioning, credit.

It feeds back into:

- Market layer through flows, positioning, and risk appetite.
- Corporate layer through cost of capital.
- Social layer through wealth effects.
- Central bank layer through financial conditions.

### Common mistakes

- Jumping from a headline directly to a trade without mapping the transmission path.
- Treating a macro signal as equally important for every asset.
- Ignoring time horizon: a short-term risk-off shock and a long-term productivity boom can coexist.
- Confusing evidence strength with personal conviction.
- Forgetting to define invalidation conditions.

## Final Integration Principle

No single layer explains the market alone. A robust analysis asks:

1. What happened?
2. Which layer did it directly affect?
3. Which nodes changed?
4. Through which channels can the effect propagate?
5. Is the effect short-term, medium-term, long-term, or structural?
6. What evidence supports this?
7. What evidence could disprove it?
